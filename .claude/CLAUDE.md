# CLAUDE.md — InsuranceExit.us

This file is the source of truth for Claude when working on the InsuranceExit.us content site. Read this in full before producing, editing, or reviewing any page.

---

## 1. Project overview

**Site name:** InsuranceExit.us

**What it is:** A content site for US homeowners who have just received — or fear receiving — a homeowners insurance non-renewal letter due to climate risk.

**What it is not:** An affiliate site. A lead-gen funnel. A quote engine. A chatbot. A real-estate-optimism blog. An insurance-industry mouthpiece.

**v1 scope:** Pure structured, trustworthy information. No AI tools, chatbots, or "get a quote" features. No product pushes.

---

## 2. Audience

- **Age:** 45–70
- **Situation:** Owns a mortgaged home in a high-risk state
- **High-risk states (priority order):** Florida, California, Louisiana, Texas, Arizona, Colorado, New Mexico, Idaho, Montana
- **Emotional state:** Reading with anxiety. They have 30–90 days to make decisions worth $200k+.
- **Sophistication:** Smart but not insurance-literate. They know what a mortgage is. They do not know what a "surplus lines carrier" is until you tell them.

Every page should assume the reader is stressed, time-pressured, and skeptical. Respect their intelligence. Do not waste their time.

---

## 3. Voice and tone

- **Calm.** The reader is panicking; the page is not.
- **Plain-English.** Anti-jargon. If an industry term is unavoidable, define it on first use in parentheses.
- **Skeptical of insurance companies.** They are not the villain, but they are not the reader's friend either.
- **Skeptical of real-estate optimism.** "It'll all work out" is not analysis.
- **No hype. No selling. No affiliate energy.**
- **Reference point:** Consumer Reports meets Bogleheads. A trusted, no-nonsense friend giving you the straight story.

Tone test before publishing: would a 62-year-old in Fort Myers who just got non-renewed feel respected, informed, and less alone after reading this? If yes, ship. If no, rewrite.

---

## 4. Mandatory requirements (non-negotiable)

### 4.1 Disclaimer on every page

Every page — without exception — must display this disclaimer prominently at both the **top** and the **bottom** of the page:

> *This site provides general information only and is not legal, financial, or insurance advice. Consult licensed professionals for your specific situation.*

### 4.2 Authoritative source citations

In every relevant article, prominently reference both of these reports by their full name:

1. **U.S. Treasury Federal Insurance Office (FIO), January 2025 report:** *Analyses of U.S. Homeowners Insurance Markets, 2018–2022: Climate-Related Risks and Other Factors*
2. **U.S. Senate Budget Committee, December 2024 staff report:** *"Next to Fall: The Climate-Driven Insurance Crisis Is Here — And Getting Worse"*

Cite by name in-line, not just in a footer. Reader should never wonder where a number came from.

### 4.3 Supplementary data sources

Supplement the two reports above with the most recent publicly available data from:

- NAIC (National Association of Insurance Commissioners)
- State insurance departments (FL OIR, CA CDI, LA LDI, TX TDI, etc.)
- Insurer 10-K filings (SEC EDGAR)
- IBHS (Insurance Institute for Business & Home Safety) for fortification standards
- FEMA, NOAA, USGS for hazard data
- Citizens Property Insurance, California FAIR Plan, Louisiana Citizens public reports

### 4.4 Date every statistic

Every single number gets a date. Format: **"As of Q1 2026…"** or **"As of March 2026…"** Never publish a floating, undated statistic.

### 4.5 Formatting

- Use tables, simple decision trees, and bullet-point comparisons **liberally**.
- Article length: **1,800–2,800 words**.
- Clear subheads. Scannable structure. The anxious reader skims first, reads second.

## 5. Site taxonomy (four pillars)

These are the four top-level navigation items. Every article belongs to exactly one pillar.

| Pillar | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **I just got non-renewed** | Immediate decision trees and checklists. For readers in the first 72 hours of crisis. |
| **Is my ZIP next** | State-by-state and ZIP-level risk explainers. For readers who haven't been hit yet but see the writing on the wall. |
| **Fortifying vs. selling** | Honest cost-benefit analysis, IBHS FORTIFIED standards, retrofit ROI. Genuinely even-handed. |
| **Residual markets** | FAIR Plan, Citizens, Louisiana Citizens, etc., explained state by state. |

### Pillar-specific rules

- **Fortifying vs. selling** must be genuinely even-handed. Present real numbers on both sides. Do not push one outcome. If the math favors selling for a given scenario, say so. If it favors fortifying, say so. Show the work either way.

## 7. State-page master template (page 10)

The state-page template must include, at minimum, these sections:

1. **The one-paragraph summary** — what's happening to homeowners insurance in this state, dated.
2. **Non-renewal and cancellation rates** — most recent NAIC + state DOI data, with dates.
3. **Major carriers leaving, pausing, or shrinking** — table form, with the date of each announcement.
4. **The residual market option in this state** — what it covers, what it doesn't, how to apply.
5. **Top hazards driving the crisis** — wildfire, hurricane, hail, flood, freeze — ranked by frequency and severity for this state.
6. **What state regulators have done** — recent rate filings, moratoriums, market-stabilization laws.
7. **Fortification programs available** — state grants, IBHS FORTIFIED uptake, premium discounts mandated.
8. **What homeowners are reporting** — qualitative, sourced from public state DOI complaint data or news reporting.
9. **Three things to do in the next 30 days** — concrete, dated, plain-English.
10. **Sources and date of last update.**

The template must be structurally identical across all state pages so a reader can compare states quickly.

## 8. Jargon protocol

Default rule: do not use insurance-industry jargon. When unavoidable, define on first use, in plain English, in parentheses or a short clause.

Examples:

- **Non-renewal** — when your insurer decides not to offer you a new policy when your current one ends. This is different from cancellation (which happens mid-policy) and different from you choosing to switch.
- **Surplus lines carrier** — an insurance company that isn't licensed in your state the normal way. It can offer coverage the standard market won't, but with fewer consumer protections.
- **Wind/hail deductible** — a separate, usually higher deductible that only applies to wind or hail damage. Often expressed as a percentage of your home's insured value, not a dollar amount.
- **Assignment of Benefits (AOB)** — a form that lets a contractor deal with your insurance company directly. Heavily abused in Florida; restricted by recent state laws.

Maintain a running glossary page. Every defined term on a content page also lives in the glossary.

## 9. Article structure (default template)

Every article should follow this skeleton unless there is a strong reason to deviate:

1. **Top disclaimer** (mandatory, verbatim).
2. **One-sentence summary** of what the reader will get out of this page.
3. **The fast answer** — for the reader who has 60 seconds. Bullet points or a short table.
4. **The fuller picture** — subheaded sections, 1,800–2,800 words total.
5. **Data table or decision tree** — at least one visual structure per article.
6. **What to do in the next 30 days** — three to five concrete, plain-English steps.
7. **Sources** — list of cited reports, filings, and data, with the date each was published or accessed.
8. **Last updated** — date.
9. **Bottom disclaimer** (mandatory, verbatim).

---

## 10. What this site never does

- Never recommends a specific insurance carrier.
- Never recommends a specific real-estate agent, contractor, or lawyer.
- Never includes affiliate links, "compare quotes" widgets, lead-gen forms, or AI chatbots in v1.
- Never tells a reader to sell. Never tells a reader to stay.
- Never reproduces copyrighted material verbatim. Paraphrase reports and cite them by full name.
- Never hypes. Never minimizes. Never pretends the crisis is over.
