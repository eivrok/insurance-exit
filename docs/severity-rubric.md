# State severity rubric — InsuranceExit.org

The severity badge on each state page (the `severity` prop on `StatePage.astro`,
rendered as "Market Crisis" / "High Stress" / "Elevated Risk") measures **current
market stress for a homeowner trying to obtain, keep, or afford coverage today** —
NOT long-term structural hazard.

A hurricane- or wildfire-exposed state that has stabilized rates and restored
coverage availability rates *lower* than a state where homeowners currently
cannot get covered. The reader is in crisis now; the badge answers "how bad is it
for me, right now, here?"

Review every quarterly stat-refresh (see `stat-refresh.md`).

## critical — "Market Crisis"
The admitted market has broken down. Meets one or more, and the trend is
deteriorating or not yet stabilized:
- Mass non-renewals or insolvencies still ongoing (not just historical).
- The residual market (FAIR Plan / Citizens) functions as a *primary* insurer —
  growing, not a true last resort.
- Many ZIP codes have no admitted-market option at any price.
- An active or recent residual-market assessment ("hurricane tax" / "FAIR Plan
  tax") is being levied.

## high — "High Stress"
Widespread, significant strain, but coverage is still obtainable (if expensive).
Meets several:
- Sharp premium increases (broadly double-digit above pre-crisis levels).
- Meaningful, widespread non-renewals; carriers pulling back from high-risk zones.
- Residual market growing or strained, OR no residual backstop exists at all.
- High uninsured rate.

Includes two cases: states **recovering from** critical (rates easing but still
high-cost), and states with severe structural exposure but a still-functioning
market.

## elevated — "Elevated Risk"
Localized or emerging stress; the market is broadly functional:
- Non-renewals concentrated in specific WUI / hazard ZIP zones, not statewide.
- Premiums rising but from a lower base; coverage generally available.
- No mass-market breakdown.
